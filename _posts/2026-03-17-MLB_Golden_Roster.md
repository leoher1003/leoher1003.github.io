## Project Overview
A popular baseball adage states that "pitching and defense win championships." However, modern baseball focuses on building the most tight 26-man roster possible, but will this guarantee the World Series?

This project analyzes 14 years of MLB data (excluding the shortened 2020 season) to statistically shape the conditions of being the Champion by applying an economic concept "Gini coefficient" to WAR (Wins Above Replacement), and will utilize machine learning (Random Forest) using WAR ratios of the top 3 players, starting pitchers, relief pitchers and batters to unmask the differences between a good regular season team and a championship winning team.

## Data & Methodology
- *Data Source:* [FanGraphs](https://www.fangraphs.com/) via the `pybaseball` library.
    - *MLB Standings & Payroll:* [Kaggle MLB Payroll Dataset](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/christophertreasure/mlb-team-payrolls-2011-2024/data)
- *Sample Size:* 420 Team-Seasons (30 teams, 14 seasons)
- *Key Metrics Engineered:*
    1. **WAR Gini Coefficient:** This economic concept is originally used to determine the income disparity, where a higher Gini means high inequality, and vice versa. In the baseball context, a high Gini coefficient will mean that the team relies on a few superstars. WAR (Wins Above Replacement) refers to the player's total value to their team in wins compared to a readily available "replacement-level" player. In here, a team made of replacement-level players is projected to record around 0.294 to 0.320 winning percentage. 
    2. **Top 3 WAR Ratio:** Measures the percentage of total team WAR generated by the top 3 players.
    3. **Positional WAR Ratios:** The percentage of total team WAR coming from Batters, Starting Pitchers (SP), and Relief Pitchers (RP).

## Key Findings & Visualizations
### 1. The regular season demands depth.
![WAR Gini vs wins](/assets/images/championship_roster_analysis_fixed.png)
Looking at the trendline of all 420 teams, there is a clear negative correlation between the WAR Gini coefficient and Regular Season wins.
From this, it can be interpreted that **To Survive the 162-game marathon, depth is strictly required**. 

However, the Champions (Red Stars) are widely dispersed, where teams with low depth will struggle less since an elite talent can dominate a short series (e.g., the 2012 SF Giants)

Top 3 Player Reliance shows a similar negative trend, which is equivalent to the analysis of WAR Gini coefficient. In contrast, only one WS winning team had a ratio higher than 0.5, which indicates that having three superstars cannot guarantee Championship. 

### 2. The positional cheat code: The bullpen premium
![Positional Balance](/assets/images/positional_scatter_analysis.png)
Analysis using WAR coefficient tells us how WAR is distributed, not where. The scatterplot shows a striking pattern shown in the Champions, where SP and Batters take the majority of the team's WAR, where the Bullpen (RP) ratio is skewed towards the Champions. This indicates that having a strong SP rotation and Batters are a 'must-have' for Championship winning teams in order to survive the 162-game marathon, but having a strong Bullpen (RP) is not as critical in the regular season compared to other positions.

## Machine Learning Verification
To eliminate human bias and statistically verify which roster structure metric is the strongest predictor of a Championship, I trained a **Random Forest Classifier**.
![Feature Importance](/assets/images/ml_feature_importance.png)

**Addressing Data Imbalance:** Predicting WS winners is highly imbalanced (only 14 champions out of 420, ~3.3%). To prevent the model from predicting "0" for every team, the `class_weight='balanced'` parameter was applied to penalize misclassifications of the minority class.

**The Surprising Result: The Constant vs. The Variable**
The algorithm concluded that the #1 determining factor was neither the Gini Coefficient nor the Bullpen—it was the **Batter WAR Ratio**. 
While a hot starting pitcher or lock-down bullpen swings a short series, pitchers only impact a fraction of the innings. Position players are on the field for every single pitch. Because FanGraphs' Batter WAR heavily incorporates *Defense* and *Base Running*, the ML model proves a fundamental truth: Pitching may get you to October, but a relentless everyday lineup and rock-solid defense form the indisputable backbone of a Champion.

## Conclusion
1. Depth is crucial for the 162-game marathon.

As seen in the Gini coefficient visualization, having a low-Gini roster is beneficial in securing a playoff ticket in general. Also, relying on a few superstars are inefficient, as the Top 3 Player ratio shows that high reliance on few players are not efficient.

2. SP and Batters are crucial in the regular season, but Bullpens are critical for short seires success.

Although the positional scatter shows that most of the team WAR should be constructed by Starting pitchers and Batters, the Random Forest result shows that Bullpen(RP) is the second important factor in winning the World Series.

3. Batters can play everyday, but pitchers cannot.

Batters can contribute in every single game of the season, and in multiple ways. A batter's WAR is comprised of batting, baserunning, and fielding, pitchers' WAR are highly dependent on their pitching statistics. As WAR is a cumulative statistic, batters are advantageous in having higher WAR than pitchers.


## Limitations

1. The unpredictability of short series compared to WAR
2. Small sample size, where there are only 1 champions and 29 non-champion teams, making 14 qualifying samples and 406 that do not.
3. This analysis ignores unquantifiable features of baseball, such as the ability of the Manager, size of the fanbase and clubhouse chemistry.
